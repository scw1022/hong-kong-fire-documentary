      Toronto man watched childhood home burn in Hong Kong fire

      Toronto resident Paul Chow was devastated when the apartment where he grew up made international news this week after a raging inferno tore through seven highrise towers in Hong Kong, leaving more than 100 dead and hundreds missing.

      The blaze at an eight-block housing complex has killed more than 100 people

      Toronto resident Paul Chow was devastated when the apartment where he grew up made international news this week after a raging inferno tore through seven highrise towers in Hong Kong, leaving more than 100 dead and hundreds missing.

      It has been a roller-coaster of emotions for Chow, who has gone from worry to sadness, distress and anger as he watched in horror, seeing the place he was born and lived for more than 30 years engulfed in flames, and knowing many of his former neighbours were still unaccounted for.

      "I want to cry and feel speechless," said Chow, adding the tragedy has kept him up at night.

      The deadly fire ripped through much of the eight-block Wang Fuk Court housing complex in Hong Kong's Tai Po district on Wednesday.

      The blaze jumped rapidly from one building to the next as foam panels and bamboo scaffolding covered in green mesh netting installed by a construction company caught fire.

      Hong Kong Fire Services says it took firefighters a day to get the fire under control, noting it was fully extinguished by Friday morning, about 40 hours after it started.

      Chow was a former councillor of Tai Po District in Hong Kong before moving to Toronto in 2022. He said the apartment complex is a little more than 40 years old, and most of its residents are seniors.

      Chow says his parents and his sister live on the seventh floor in one of the buildings, and he rushed to get in touch with them immediately as soon as he saw the news.

      Fortunately, Chow says his parents were on vacation when the fire broke out, and his sister was at work.

      Hong Kong firefighters found dozens more bodies on Friday in an intensive search of a highrise apartment complex. The death toll rose to 128, and many remain unaccounted for. Authorities arrested eight people involved in the apartment towers' renovation, bringing the total to 11.

      He says that although his loved ones are safe and now temporarily staying at a family member's home, his parents still aren't allowed to go inside to check the conditions of the unit.

      Before the fire, the Wang Fuk Court complex had been under renovation. Exterior walls of the highrises had been shrouded in green mesh netting and bamboo scaffolding. 

      Officials in Hong Kong said they are now investigating the materials used — both the netting on the scaffolding and the foam panels covering windows — to see whether they contributed to the fire.

      Chow said the inferno has left his family heartbroken. They are also frustrated as they just put money toward the renovation before the fire. 

      "We paid more than 100,000 Hong Kong dollars (about C$17,952) for the construction, and it caught on fire and many people lost their lives," said Chow with red, tear-filled eyes. 

      “This is very ridiculous, and I don't even know why this could have happened in a modern society like Hong Kong."

      Accountability needed, community says

      Chow says he believes this is a human-caused incident, and he said this "disaster in Hong Kong history" could have been avoided.

      Chow says he hopes the people and companies who are responsible for the tragedy will be held accountable, adding that the Hong Kong government should take more action to ensure affected residents have a warm place to stay as they await compensation.

      "The most important thing is still to find out the truth," said Chow, adding he appreciates the world's attention as it will keep pressure on local authorities. 

      Dominic Tse is the priest at North York Christian Community Church, where Chow attends. He said many people in the church's community have family and loved ones affected by the fire and they want accountability.

      "They should have an independent investigation to find out exactly what happened, who are responsible..." said Tse. "I hope that will be a turning point from a disaster to something that can be prevented in the future."

      Some community members' loved ones died in the blaze. Tse said that was on his mind while giving his sermon on Sunday. He said he talked about hope.

      "People were saying, 'my family died and it's just disaster.' Christmas season is a time we have hope," he said.

      Hong Kong started a three-day official mourning period from Saturday to Monday to remember those killed in the fire.

      Global Affairs Canada estimates there are about 300,000 Canadians living in Hong Kong. The agency said it's not aware of any Canadian citizens affected by the fire in Hong Kong.

      "Canada is deeply saddened by this tragic fire. We extend our heartfelt condolences to the families and loved ones of those who lost their lives, and wish a full and speedy recovery to all those injured," read the statement.
